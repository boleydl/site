General Information
This course introduces the basic numerical techniques to solve mathematical problems on a digital computer. Algorithms for several common problems encountered in computer science, mathematics, science and engineering are introduced. The pitfalls and errors that can arise when solving mathematical problems with methods taking finite time and in finite precision arithmetic are discussed, and measures to predict when such pitfalls are encountered will be introduced.

Computer Platform

Students will be expected to implement several of the algorithms on a digital computer using python with a limited set of packages (e.g., numpy, matplotlib, pdb, plus a few functions listed explicitly from other packages). In the cases where another equivalent interactive programming environment is allowed, you will be responsible for implementing the equivalent features and may find only limited help from the instructional staff. Students should be familiar with basic programming techniques. Students should also be acquainted with the basic concepts of the more elementary mathematical and numerical methods (e.g. solving simple linear equations, root-finding, computing averages, using derivatives to find the minimum of a scalar function, etc.) though some of this will be reviewed during the course.

Ethics

All items handed in to be graded must represent the individual effort of whoever's name(s) appears on the item. At a minimum, violators of this policy may fail the course and/or may have their names recorded at appropriate University or Departmental offices. Mutual discussion of each individual's results in the homeworks is encouraged, as long as the results themselves represent individual efforts. If you use or submit any material or software you obtained from the WWW, any chatbot or LLM, or any other source outside of class or the textbooks, you must cite it including an explanation of how you used that outside source. In some assignments, you may be restricted on what software you can use. If there are any questions about the source of your answers, you may be asked to explain your understanding of the answers in person. This may occur if there is a consistent mismatch between your performance on in-class items versus take-home submissions.

Assignments and Grading

Some assignments may be graded in an appreviated manner with credit for some questions being awarded based just on a quick look for the presence of proper answers. Some assignments may be assigned to be done by pairs of students; such items should be handed in as a single item listing the names of all participants. To pass the course at any level, you will have to achieve a passing grade at a commensurate level on the exams alone, and do satisfactorily on the homeworks. Any questions about the grading of any item must be asked within a week of when items are first handed back to students. After one week has passed, the scores become final. Exams are in class with no electronic devices allowed. You can bring and refer to anything on paper such as class notes printed in advance, a crib sheet, etc. A simple calculator with no wireless connection capability will be allowed, though some effort will be made to keep any necessary arithmetic as simple as possible. The above policies do not apply to in-class classroom exercises during which talk among yourselves and accessing the network will be allowed and even encouraged. A good-faith effort to answer the questions is sufficient for full credit, as long as the answers are handed in at the end of the class period. Anything handed in later for any reason whatsoever (even if legitimate) will get only partial credit based on the correctness of the answers. However you are allowed to miss up to two clasroom exercises during the semester without any penalty.

Late Homework Submissions

Homeworks will be accepted until answers have been posted or discussed in class or up to three working days after the due date (whichever occurs first). For regular homeworks, the late penalty will grow nonlinearly with the delay: 1% if within 12 hours ( only once during the semester), 2% if within 24 hours, 10% up to 2 days, 20% if more. The penalty will be doubled for items with due dates within 1 day of the posting date, and anything due in the last week of class. The score posted for any assignment might not include any late penalty, which may be imposed and recorded later as a separate item. If unable to complete a quiz during class, you should hand in what you can in class, and hand in your more complete answer by 11:59pm the same day. Quizzes handed during class will be scored based on a good faith effort. Quizzes handed in later for any reason (including legitimate ones) will be scored on correctness and receive only partial credit. We suggest you photograph your in-class quiz with your cell phone before handing it in. Answers to homeworks and quizzes could be posted any time after the due date/time without advance notice, after which no more submissions will be accepted. Whether or not a per-assignment penalty is applied or waived based on a reasonable and unanticipated excuse, the final semester accumulated score will be reduced by 1 point for each item handed in late.
